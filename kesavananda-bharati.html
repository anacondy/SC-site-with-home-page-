The Kesavananda Bharati case is arguably the most significant constitutional case in Indian judicial history. The 13-judge Constitution Bench established the doctrine of the "Basic Structure" of the Constitution, holding that while Parliament has wide powers to amend the Constitution under Article 368, it cannot alter or destroy the Constitution's basic structure or essential features.

The case arose when Kesavananda Bharati, the head of a Hindu mutt in Kerala, challenged the Kerala Land Reforms Act and constitutional amendments that sought to curtail fundamental rights. The judgment was delivered after 68 days of hearing, making it one of the longest hearings in Supreme Court history.

ARGUMENTS

Petitioner's Case (Kesavananda Bharati)

  1. Parliament's power to amend the Constitution under Article 368 is not unlimited and cannot destroy fundamental rights.
  2. The 24th, 25th, and 29th Amendments violate the basic structure of the Constitution.
  3. Fundamental rights form the core of the Constitution and cannot be abrogated by amendments.
  4. The Kerala Land Reforms Act violates the right to property and equality before law.

Respondent's Case (State of Kerala & Union of India)

  1. Parliament has absolute and unlimited power to amend any part of the Constitution, including fundamental rights.
  2. Article 368 does not impose any inherent limitations on the amending power.
  3. The amendments were enacted to give effect to the Directive Principles of State Policy.
  4. Land reforms are essential for social justice and redistribution of wealth.

The Court's decision was nuanced and complex. While it overruled Golak Nath and upheld Parliament's power to amend fundamental rights, it simultaneously imposed a significant limitation: amendments cannot alter the Constitution's basic structure. This doctrine has since become a cornerstone of Indian constitutional law.

Though the majority agreed on the basic structure doctrine, there was disagreement on what constitutes the basic structure. The judgment identified several features including supremacy of the Constitution, republican and democratic form of government, secular character, separation of powers, and federal character of the Constitution.

COURT'S ANALYSIS

The Court analyzed the relationship between Article 368 (amending power) and Article 13 (laws inconsistent with fundamental rights). The majority held that while Article 368 confers constituent power on Parliament, this power is subject to inherent limitations. The Constitution is not merely a statute but the supreme law embodying certain inviolable core principles. The Court reasoned that the Constitution's founding fathers intended to create a permanent structure with essential features that should endure. The power to amend cannot be construed as a power to destroy or abrogate these essential features. This interpretation balanced the need for constitutional flexibility with the preservation of fundamental constitutional values, ensuring that temporary political majorities cannot fundamentally alter the nation's constitutional identity.

FINAL VERDICT

  • Parliament has the power to amend any provision of the Constitution, including fundamental rights.
  • However, Parliament cannot alter or destroy the basic structure of the Constitution.
  • The 24th Amendment Act, 1971 is valid.
  • The 25th Amendment Act, 1971 is valid, except for the clause ousting judicial review.
  • The basic structure doctrine acts as a limitation on Parliament's amending power.

RATIO DECIDENDI

While Parliament possesses wide powers to amend the Constitution under Article 368, including fundamental rights, it cannot exercise this power to damage, emasculate, destroy, abrogate, or alter the basic structure or essential features of the Constitution. Any amendment that violates the basic structure is void and unconstitutional. The doctrine of basic structure is a judicial innovation to preserve constitutional supremacy and prevent arbitrary amendments.
